Bankruptcy Discharge

The Legal Order That Eliminates Your Debt

What Is a Bankruptcy Discharge?

A bankruptcy discharge is a court order that permanently eliminates your personal obligation to pay certain debts. Once a debt is discharged, the creditor can never collect it from you -- no calls, no letters, no lawsuits, no garnishment. It is the legal fresh start that bankruptcy provides.

When Do You Receive the Discharge?

Chapter 7: Approximately 60 days after the 341 meeting of creditors (roughly 4 months after filing).

Chapter 13: After completing all payments under the plan (3-5 years after filing).

Chapter 11: Upon confirmation of the plan or completion of plan payments, depending on the type of plan.

What Debts Are Discharged?

Most unsecured debts: credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, past-due utility bills, lease obligations, deficiency balances from repossession or foreclosure, and most lawsuit judgments.

What Debts Survive Bankruptcy?

Some debts are non-dischargeable: most student loans (absent undue hardship), taxes from the last 3 years, child support and alimony, debts from fraud or willful injury, criminal fines and restitution, and DUI-related debts. Full list of non-dischargeable debts under Section 523(a).

The Discharge Injunction (Section 524)

The discharge order creates a permanent injunction under Section 524 of the Bankruptcy Code. Any creditor who attempts to collect a discharged debt is violating a federal court order and can be held in contempt. This includes sending bills, making phone calls, filing lawsuits, or reporting the debt as owing on credit reports. Section 524 explained.

Discharge Denial and Revocation

A discharge can be denied if: you committed fraud, concealed assets, destroyed records, or failed to complete the debtor education course. A discharge can be revoked within 1 year if obtained through fraud. Discharge bar periods.

After the Discharge

Check your credit reports -- all discharged debts should show $0 balance. Begin rebuilding credit with a secured card. Your obligation to pay discharged debts is permanently eliminated. Credit rebuilding guide.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is a bankruptcy discharge permanent?

Yes. Once granted, the discharge permanently eliminates your obligation to pay the discharged debts, with very limited exceptions for fraud.

Can a creditor still contact me after discharge?

No. Any attempt to collect a discharged debt violates the discharge injunction and can result in contempt of court sanctions.

Do I need to do anything after receiving my discharge?

Check your credit reports to ensure discharged debts show $0 balance. Report any violations to your bankruptcy attorney or the court.

What is a bankruptcy discharge?

A bankruptcy discharge is a permanent court order under 11 U.S.C. Section 524 that eliminates your personal obligation to pay certain debts. After discharge, creditors cannot collect, sue, garnish wages, or contact you about discharged debts. It is the legal fresh start that bankruptcy provides.

How long does it take to get a discharge?

In Chapter 7, you typically receive a discharge approximately 60 days after the 341 meeting of creditors, which means roughly four months after filing. In Chapter 13, the discharge is entered after you complete all plan payments over three to five years.

What debts are included in a bankruptcy discharge?

Most unsecured debts are discharged including credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, utility arrears, and old lease obligations. Debts not discharged include most student loans, recent taxes, child support, alimony, criminal fines, and debts obtained through fraud under 11 U.S.C. Section 523.

Can a creditor collect on a discharged debt?

No. Any attempt to collect a discharged debt violates the discharge injunction under 11 U.S.C. Section 524 and can result in contempt of court sanctions, actual damages, attorney fees, and in some cases punitive damages. Report violations to the bankruptcy court immediately.

What is the difference between discharge and dismissal?

A discharge permanently eliminates your debts and is the successful outcome of bankruptcy. A dismissal ends the case without eliminating any debts, restoring all creditor collection rights. Dismissed cases provide no debt relief and the debtor still owes every dollar. The two outcomes are fundamentally different.
